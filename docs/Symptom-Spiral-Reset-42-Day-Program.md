# The Symptom Spiral Reset
### A 42-Day Program to Stop Compulsive Symptom-Googling, Reassurance-Seeking, and Body-Checking

**Core promise:** In 42 days, break the loop that keeps health worry alive — not by getting more certainty about your body, but by learning to need less of it.

---

## Read This First (Important)

**What this is:** A structured, psychoeducational self-help program built on the same Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Exposure & Response Prevention (ERP) methods that clinicians use for health anxiety, with elements of Acceptance & Commitment Therapy (ACT). It is a *training program for a habit pattern* — not a treatment for a disease, and not a chatbot.

**What this is not:** It is **not therapy, not medical advice, and not a diagnostic tool.** It does not replace a doctor, therapist, or psychiatrist. It complements professional care. If your health worry is severe, has lasted years, comes with depression, or significantly disrupts your life, please do this program *alongside* a licensed therapist, not instead of one.

**If you are in crisis** — having thoughts of harming yourself, or feeling unable to stay safe — stop and contact your local emergency number or a crisis line now. This program is not designed for crisis support.

**This program does not ask you to ignore your health.** Recovery from health anxiety is *appropriate medical care minus the compulsions* — not the absence of care. On Day 6 you'll set up a sensible health plan and a list of genuine red-flag symptoms that always warrant prompt medical attention. The target here is the *excess* — the 3am Googling, the 40-times-a-day pulse checks, the repeated "do you think this is serious?" texts — never reasonable self-care.

**Medical clearance:** Some Week 4 exercises deliberately bring on harmless sensations (a faster heartbeat, lightheadedness). If you have a diagnosed heart, lung, neurological, or balance condition, are pregnant, or are unsure for any reason, check with your doctor first and skip any exercise that isn't safe for your body. Every such exercise is clearly flagged.

## How the Spiral Works (The Loop You're Breaking)

Health anxiety runs on a loop. Once you can see it, you can interrupt it.

1. **Trigger** — a bodily sensation, a health headline, someone's diagnosis, a memory.
2. **Catastrophic interpretation** — "this twinge means something is seriously wrong."
3. **Anxiety spikes** — and anxiety itself produces *more* real sensations (racing heart, tight chest, dizziness), which feed step 2.
4. **Safety behavior** — you Google, check your body, seek reassurance, or avoid.
5. **Short-term relief** — for about four minutes. Then the doubt returns, often stronger.
6. **The loop reinforces itself** — every check teaches your brain that checking was *necessary*, so the urge grows more frequent.

The engine underneath all of it is **intolerance of uncertainty** — the need to *know for sure* you're okay. You can never get that certainty about your body. Nobody can. The way out isn't to win the certainty game — it's to **stop playing it**, to learn to carry "I can't be 100% sure, and I can live fully anyway."

The program dismantles the loop in a deliberate order: first you *see* it, then you *loosen the thoughts*, then you *stop feeding it* (response prevention), then you *face the fears* (exposure), then you *build tolerance for uncertainty*, then you *make the gains permanent.*

## How the Program Works

- **42 days, 6 phases of 7 days each.** 15–25 minutes a day.
- **Every day has the same three parts:** a short **Idea** (what to understand), a **Practice** (what to do), and the **Daily Check-In** (what to log). The Idea and Practice change daily; the Check-In is identical every day so your progress is measurable.
- **Three behavioral targets** run through everything: **Googling**, **reassurance-seeking**, and **body-checking.**
- **Your Spiral Score** (below) turns daily check-ins into one number you watch fall over six weeks.
- **Go at your pace, but daily.** Consistency beats intensity. A hard day done beats a perfect day skipped. If a day feels like too much, drop down a rung — don't drop out.

## The Spiral Score (Your Progress System)

Every evening, complete the **Daily Check-In** and compute today's **Spiral Score (0–100, lower is better)**. The score is engineered so that *resisting urges lowers it* — so you can have a high-anxiety day and still "win" by not acting on the urges. That is exactly what recovery looks like: not the absence of fear, but the presence of resistance.

**Daily Check-In — log these 7 every evening:**
1. Peak anxiety today (0–10)
2. Time lost to health worry (none / under 15 min / 15–60 min / 1–3 hrs / over 3 hrs)
3. Symptom-Google episodes (count)
4. Reassurance requests, to people or doctors (count)
5. Body-checking episodes (count)
6. **Urges you noticed and did *not* act on** (count — your recovery metric)
7. Did health fear make you avoid anything today? (yes/no + what)

**Spiral Score formula:**

```
Compulsions  = (Googles + Reassurance + Body-checks) × 4     (capped at 40)
Distress     = Peak anxiety (0–10) × 3                        (0–30)
Time lost    = none:0 | <15m:5 | 15–60m:10 | 1–3h:15 | >3h:20 (0–20)
Avoidance    = no:0 | yes:10                                  (0–10)

RAW          = Compulsions + Distress + Time lost + Avoidance (0–100)

Resilience   = (Urges resisted × 2, capped at 20)
             + (5 if you completed today's exercise/exposure, else 0)

SPIRAL SCORE = RAW − Resilience           (floor at 0)
```

**Worked example.** A rough day: anxiety peaked at 8; you Googled twice and checked your pulse three times (5 compulsions); lost ~2 hours; avoided nothing; but you noticed and resisted 6 urges and did the day's exposure.
→ Compulsions 5×4 = 20. Distress 8×3 = 24. Time = 15. Avoidance = 0. **RAW = 59.** Resilience = 12 + 5 = 17. **Spiral Score = 42.** A genuinely anxious day, scored as real progress — because you fought back.

**Tracking cadence:**
- **Baseline** = your average Spiral Score for Days 1–7 (Week 1 is observation; you measure, you don't yet change behavior).
- **Checkpoints** = Week 3 average (Day 21, halfway) and Week 6 average (Day 42, finish).
- **The trajectory is the story** — watch the weekly average fall.

**Optional gold-standard measure.** For a rigorous before/after, take a validated questionnaire — the **Short Health Anxiety Inventory (SHAI)** — on Day 0, Day 21, and Day 42 and compare. (Source it via a clinician or a reputable published version and follow its scoring. The Spiral Score is a custom progress tracker built for this program; the SHAI is a clinically validated instrument and gives a more defensible outcome.)

# PHASE 1 — UNDERSTAND THE SPIRAL
### Days 1–7 · Goal: see the loop clearly, measure your baseline, change nothing yet

> This week you're a scientist studying your own pattern. **Don't try to stop checking yet** — forcing change before you understand the loop usually backfires. Watch, log, build the map. Behavior change starts in Week 3.

**Day 1 — Map your spiral.**
*Idea:* Every spiral has the same five links: trigger → catastrophic thought → anxiety → safety behavior → brief relief → rebound. Naming the links removes their automatic power.
*Practice:* Write out your most recent spiral in full, link by link. What set it off? What was the exact catastrophic thought? What did you do? How long did the relief last before doubt returned? Then write down, as best you recall, **yesterday's baseline counts**: how many times did you Google a symptom, check your body, and ask for reassurance?

**Day 2 — Inventory your safety behaviors.**
*Idea:* Checking, Googling, reassurance-seeking, and avoidance are all "safety behaviors." They feel protective. They are the fuel.
*Practice:* Make a complete inventory under four headings — *Googling/searching*, *body-checking* (pulse, lumps, skin, throat, breathing…), *reassurance-seeking* (family, partner, doctors, forums), *avoidance* (places, content, words, activities you dodge). Be exhaustive. You can't dismantle what you can't see.

**Day 3 — The four-minute fix.**
*Idea:* Reassurance works — for about four minutes. Then relief decays and doubt returns, usually stronger. This is *negative reinforcement*: brief relief trains the brain to seek the behavior again, harder.
*Practice:* Next time you check or Google today, time the relief. Note the moment calm arrives and the moment the first new doubt appears. Write down the "relief half-life." Most people are shocked how short it is.

**Day 4 — Your body is noisy.**
*Idea:* A healthy body produces dozens of harmless sensations an hour — twitches, gurgles, aches, flutters, pressures. People without health anxiety don't notice most of them. Anxiety doesn't invent sensations so much as *spotlight* them — then add real ones of its own.
*Practice:* Set a 5-minute timer and do a "sensation census": list every physical sensation you notice right now, without judging any as dangerous. Count them. This is the raw material your spiral has been treating as evidence.

**Day 5 — Theory A vs. Theory B.**
*Idea:* The most important reframe in the program. Two possible explanations for your distress:
> **Theory A:** "There is something medically wrong with me that I keep needing to find."
> **Theory B:** "There is nothing dangerous wrong — I have a *worry problem about my health* that keeps me checking."
*Practice:* For each, write: what it predicts, what supports it, and — crucially — *which better explains your actual life* (years of fears that didn't come true, normal results, the way the worry jumps from illness to illness). You don't have to *believe* Theory B yet. You only have to **live as if Theory B is true for the next 37 days** and watch what happens. That experiment *is* the program.

**Day 6 — Your sensible health plan + red flags.**
*Idea:* Recovery is appropriate care *minus* compulsion. So we define "appropriate" clearly, once — and everything above that line is the compulsion we're cutting.
*Practice:* Write your **Sensible Health Plan**: one regular doctor, your genuinely scheduled check-ups/screenings, and any monitoring a real clinician has actually told you to do. Then write your **Red-Flag list** — the small set of symptoms that warrant prompt medical attention regardless of anxiety (examples: sudden severe chest pain or pressure; difficulty breathing; sudden one-sided weakness or numbness; sudden trouble speaking or seeing; a first/worst-ever sudden headache; coughing up or passing blood; a persistent new unexplained lump; unexplained significant weight loss; fainting). Unsure what belongs? Build it *with your doctor* — that's the right kind of reassurance: sought once, in advance. From now on: red flag → act on your plan; not a red flag → it's spiral, and you'll practice letting it pass. *(Week-4 note: tell your doctor now if you have any heart, lung, neurological, balance, or pregnancy condition.)*

**Day 7 — Week 1 review + baseline.**
*Idea:* You have the map. Before changing the route, mark the start.
*Practice:* Average your Day 1–7 Spiral Scores — that's your **baseline**. Write three patterns you noticed (your most common trigger, your go-to safety behavior, your worst time of day). Recommit to living as if Theory B. *(If using SHAI, record your Day-0 score.)*

# PHASE 2 — CATCH THE CATASTROPHE
### Days 8–14 · Goal: loosen catastrophic thinking with CBT tools

> This week targets link #2: the leap from *sensation* to *catastrophe*. You're not arguing yourself into certainty (that's a reassurance trap) — you're learning to hold the thought more lightly and move on.

**Day 8 — The thought record.**
*Idea:* The catastrophe fires in a half-second, automatically. Slowing it onto paper breaks the autopilot.
*Practice:* Learn the thought record (template in Appendix B). For one worry today, write: *situation/sensation*, *automatic thought*, *emotion + intensity (0–100)*, *behavior urge*. Just capture it.

**Day 9 — Spot your distortions.**
*Idea:* Health anxiety runs on predictable traps: **catastrophizing** ("headache = tumor"), **jumping to conclusions**, **demand for certainty**, **"symptoms = serious illness,"** **emotional reasoning** ("I feel scared, so it must be dangerous").
*Practice:* Label which distortions are in yesterday's thought. Then catch and label two more in real time today. Naming the trap loosens its grip.

**Day 10 — Worst / best / most likely.**
*Idea:* The spiral lives entirely in the worst case. Widening the frame restores proportion — and builds *coping confidence*.
*Practice:* For your top fear, write the *worst*, *best*, and *most likely* case. Then answer the question that actually disarms catastrophe: **"If the worst did happen, how would I cope?"** Focus on coping, not on proving it won't happen.

**Day 11 — The courtroom (do it once).**
*Idea:* Weigh the evidence on the catastrophic thought — then drop the case and leave the courtroom. This is a one-time loosening exercise, *not* a search for certainty. Repeating it becomes reassurance.
*Practice:* In two columns, list evidence *for* and *against* the catastrophic interpretation. Write one **balanced alternative thought** you can live with ("This is a sensation my anxious brain is spotlighting; I don't need to solve it"). Read it once. Close the notebook.

**Day 12 — The Google brain.**
*Idea:* Search engines are catastrophe machines: they surface the rarest, scariest explanations, strip out base rates, and reward fear with more fear. It has a name — *cyberchondria*.
*Practice:* Pick a symptom you've Googled recently. *Without searching now*, reason it through: how common is the scary disease, really, for someone your age — versus the dozens of benign causes? The lesson isn't "see, you're fine" (that's certainty-seeking). It's: **Google distorts probability, so it can never give you the truth you're asking for.**

**Day 13 — Design a behavioral experiment.**
*Idea:* Beliefs shift faster from *testing* than from *thinking*. Your spiral makes confident predictions ("if I don't check, my anxiety will rise all day and never stop"). Predictions can be tested.
*Practice:* Write one specific, testable prediction your anxiety makes. Design a small experiment for tomorrow (e.g., "I'll resist checking my pulse tonight and record my anxiety every 30 minutes to see if it actually keeps climbing"). Predict the outcome in advance.

**Day 14 — Week 2 review.**
*Practice:* Run the experiment if you haven't, and write what actually happened vs. your prediction. Average your Week 2 Spiral Scores and compare to baseline. Note which CBT tool felt most useful — you'll keep it.

# PHASE 3 — CUT THE CHECKING
### Days 15–21 · Goal: stop feeding the loop (Exposure & Response Prevention begins)

> This is where the real change happens. You'll systematically stop the safety behaviors. Anxiety will rise at first — expected and temporary. Each urge resisted is a brick out of the spiral. **Resisted urges are the metric of the week.**

**Day 15 — Build your spiral ladder.**
*Idea:* ERP works in graded steps, not all at once: build a ladder, easiest to hardest, and climb it.
*Practice:* Using your Day-2 inventory, rate each safety behavior and feared situation by how hard it would be to resist or face, 0–100 ("difficulty"/SUDs). Order them into a ladder (template in Appendix A). This guides Weeks 3 and 4.

**Day 16 — Delay, don't obey (urge surfing).**
*Idea:* Urges are waves — they rise, crest, and fall on their own, usually within 10–30 minutes, *whether or not you act.* You don't have to ride them by checking. You can ride them by waiting.
*Practice:* Every urge to check/Google today, **delay 10 minutes** before doing anything. During the delay, name it ("this is an urge; it will pass") and let it crest. Most fade. Log every urge you sat through. Start your **resist streak** today.

**Day 17 — The body-checking fast.**
*Idea:* Body-checking (pressing for lumps, taking your pulse, inspecting skin, testing your swallow) feels like gathering information. It's actually rehearsing the fear and irritating the body — creating more sensations to check.
*Practice:* Choose your #1 body-checking compulsion from the ladder. **Don't perform it for the entire day.** When the urge comes, delay and surf. Notice the catastrophe you "needed" to check for does not arrive.

**Day 18 — The Google fast.**
*Idea:* Searching is the single biggest accelerant for most people. Cutting it produces the fastest visible drop in spiral frequency.
*Practice:* Commit to **24 hours of zero symptom-Googling.** Make it easy to keep: log out, install a site/app blocker, put your phone across the room at night. When the urge spikes, use delay + a pre-written line ("Google can't give me certainty; I'm choosing to let this pass"). Tomorrow, extend to 48 hours.

**Day 19 — End reassurance-seeking (the people version).**
*Idea:* Asking your partner, parent, or doctor "do you think this is serious?" — repeatedly — is reassurance-seeking. Their "you're fine" is the same four-minute fix as Google, and trains the loop just as hard. Even *their* willingness to reassure keeps you stuck.
*Practice:* Identify who you ask. Have a kind, direct conversation (or send the **reassurance script**, Appendix E): *"I'm working on my health anxiety. Reassurance actually makes it worse for me over time, so I'm asking you to lovingly* not *answer when I ask whether a symptom is serious — instead just remind me I'm doing my program."* Then **stop asking.**

**Day 20 — Replace the ritual.**
*Idea:* A resisted urge leaves a gap. Filling it with a chosen action makes resistance far easier than white-knuckling.
*Practice:* Pick two **response substitutions** in advance — a brief grounding practice (slow breathing, the 5-4-3-2-1 senses exercise) for the spike, and a **values action** that pulls attention outward (text a friend back, step outside, do the next thing in your day). When an urge hits: delay → substitute → carry on.

**Day 21 — Halfway review + milestone.**
*Idea:* You're halfway — the point where most people first feel the loop loosen.
*Practice:* Average Week 3 Spiral Scores; compare to baseline and Week 2. Total your resisted urges for the week — that number *is* recovery in motion. Write one thing you can now do that you couldn't on Day 1. *(If using SHAI, record Day-21 and compare to Day 0.)*

# PHASE 4 — FACE THE FEAR
### Days 22–28 · Goal: turn toward what you avoid (graded exposure)

> Response prevention removed the compulsions. Exposure now removes the *avoidance* and teaches your nervous system, through direct experience, that feared sensations and situations are uncomfortable but not dangerous. Stay on your ladder, one rung at a time. Anxiety that rises *will fall on its own* if you don't escape it — that falling curve is the whole point.

**Day 22 — Situational exposure.**
*Idea:* Avoiding hospitals, medical shows, health articles, even certain words keeps the fear oversized. Approaching them — without checking afterward — shrinks it.
*Practice:* Pick a *low-rung* avoided situation (read a general health article, watch a medical scene, walk the pharmacy aisle you skip). Do it deliberately, then **prevent the response** — no Googling or checking afterward. Rate anxiety every few minutes; watch it crest and fall. That fall is your brain learning.

**Day 23 — Interoceptive exposure (read the safety note first).**
*Idea:* For sensation-focused fears (racing heart, breathlessness, dizziness), the most powerful move is to *bring on the sensation on purpose* and let it pass without responding — proving it's harmless.
*Safety:* **Do only what's safe for your body.** Skip anything inappropriate given a heart, lung, neurological, balance, or pregnancy condition, or anything your Day-6 doctor check flagged. Stop if you feel genuinely unwell (vs. anxious).
*Practice:* Choose one safe inducer, hold ~30–60 seconds, then sit with the sensation *without checking*: e.g., **breathe quickly for 45 seconds** (lightheadedness/tingling), **jog in place or do stairs** (racing heart), or **breathe through a thin straw briefly** (breathlessness). Rate anxiety as it rises and falls. Repeat 3–4 times. The sensation is unpleasant; it is not an emergency; it passes.

**Day 24 — Interoceptive exposure, one rung up.**
*Practice:* Repeat yesterday's safe exercise slightly longer, or move to the next sensation on your ladder. The goal is *learning*, not endurance — collect evidence that the sensation arrives, peaks, and leaves on its own every time, with no checking required.

**Day 25 — Imaginal exposure (the worst-case script).**
*Idea:* The feared *thought* ("what if it really is cancer") stays powerful precisely because you keep fleeing it. Facing it deliberately, in imagination, drains its charge.
*Safety:* Intense. Pace it, do it when you have time to settle afterward, and skip or defer if you're acutely fragile or a therapist would rather guide it.
*Practice:* Write a short, first-person script of the feared outcome you most avoid imagining. Read it slowly, repeatedly, for 10–15 minutes — *without* any reassurance afterward. Rate anxiety throughout. By the fifth or sixth read, most people find the same words stop landing as hard. That habituation is the lesson.

**Day 26 — Uncertainty exposure.**
*Idea:* Underneath every health fear is the same intolerable thing: *not knowing for sure.* You can practice tolerating it directly.
*Practice:* Write and slowly repeat honest "maybe" statements: *"It's possible something is wrong with my health. I can't be completely certain. And I can carry that uncertainty and live my life anyway."* Sit with the discomfort rather than resolving it. This builds the single most important muscle in the program.

**Day 27 — Exposure + living.**
*Idea:* Recovery isn't doing exposures forever — it's living. So practice a *valued* activity that happens to trigger some health fear, fully, with no safety behaviors.
*Practice:* Choose something you want to do that anxiety has been taxing (exercise that raises your heart rate, a trip, an intimate moment, a workday without checking) and do it without checking, Googling, or seeking reassurance. Let the fear come along as a passenger, not a driver.

**Day 28 — Week 4 review.**
*Practice:* Average Week 4 Spiral Scores. Write one situation or sensation that genuinely scares you *less* now than two weeks ago, and how you know. Note any rung still hard — you'll keep practicing it.

# PHASE 5 — TOLERATE THE UNCERTAINTY
### Days 29–35 · Goal: retrain attention, contain worry, and live as if recovered

> Compulsions are down and fears face forward. Now you retrain the two background habits that keep the spiral primed — **hypervigilant body-scanning** and **uncontained worry** — and start *living* from the recovered position.

**Day 29 — Attention training.**
*Idea:* Health anxiety keeps a searchlight pointed inward at your body. Recovery widens the beam outward. Attention is trainable.
*Practice:* A 10-minute drill: pick a focus *outside* your body (sounds around you, a piece of music, a task needing your hands); each time attention snaps back to scanning your body, gently return it outward. You're not suppressing sensations — you're practicing *where* you point the light.

**Day 30 — Drop the scan.**
*Idea:* Body-scanning is itself a compulsion — a low-grade, constant one. It belongs on the response-prevention list.
*Practice:* Today, *notice* each time you scan/monitor your body, mark it, and redirect outward (the Day-29 move). Add a "scans noticed and redirected" tally to your check-in. Awareness + redirection loosens the habit.

**Day 31 — Worry postponement.**
*Idea:* You can't stop worries appearing, but you can refuse to engage them on demand. Contained worry shrinks.
*Practice:* Schedule a single 15-minute "worry window" later today. When a health worry shows up outside it, note it on a list and say, "not now — worry window." When the window arrives, most items have lost their urgency. You may choose to skip the window entirely.

**Day 32 — A full day as if recovered.**
*Idea:* You've spent five weeks building the skills. Today, run the whole pattern: live exactly as someone *without* health anxiety would for one full day.
*Practice:* No checking, no Googling, no reassurance-seeking, full engagement. Each urge: delay → substitute → carry on. In the evening, write what the day was like. This is your proof of concept for the rest of your life.

**Day 33 — Self-compassion.**
*Idea:* Shame and self-criticism ("why am I like this, it's so stupid") don't motivate recovery — they fuel anxiety and make setbacks likelier. Kindness improves follow-through.
*Practice:* Write to yourself about your health anxiety the way you'd write to a good friend who had it — acknowledging it's hard, recognizing how common it is, offering encouragement instead of judgment. Use this voice with yourself whenever you slip.

**Day 34 — Reclaim what it cost you.**
*Idea:* Health anxiety quietly bills you — missed moments, dodged activities, strained relationships, lost presence. Recovery means taking those back.
*Practice:* List what the spiral has cost you. Then choose three valued activities or connections to deliberately reclaim, and schedule the first this week. Living well *is* the long-term antidote.

**Day 35 — Week 5 review.**
*Practice:* Average Week 5 Spiral Scores. Write how your relationship with *uncertainty* has changed since Day 26 — can you hold "I don't know for sure" with less panic? Note your remaining edges.

# PHASE 6 — RECLAIM YOUR LIFE (RELAPSE-PROOF)
### Days 36–42 · Goal: lock in the gains and build a plan that outlasts the program

> A future flare-up isn't failure — it's normal, especially around real health events, stress, or others' illnesses. The difference now: you have a method. This week turns 42 days of practice into a permanent, self-running system.

**Day 36 — Map your gains.**
*Practice:* Put your Day-1 baseline next to today's: Googles, reassurance requests, body-checks, peak anxiety, time lost, avoidance. Write the change in plain language. This is the evidence Theory B was right.

**Day 37 — Know your early-warning signs.**
*Idea:* Relapse rarely arrives all at once. It creeps — one "quick" Google, one extra check, one reassurance text.
*Practice:* List *your* personal relapse triggers (a news story, a scan, a season, a stressor) and your earliest tell-tale signs the loop is restarting. Catching it early is the whole game.

**Day 38 — Your flare-up protocol.**
*Idea:* In a flare you won't want to reinvent the wheel — so write it down now.
*Practice:* Write your **5-step flare-up plan** — the exact moves when you notice a spiral starting. Example: 1) Name it as a spiral, not evidence. 2) Run the Theory A/B check. 3) Delay and surf the urge. 4) Do the response substitution. 5) If it persists past a set time or crosses your red-flag list, act on the *Sensible Health Plan* — once. Keep it where you'll find it.

**Day 39 — Reassurance-proof your environment for good.**
*Practice:* Make the temporary supports permanent: keep the site/app blockers, formalize the agreement with the people you used to ask, confirm your one-doctor, scheduled-care plan. Remove the easy on-ramps to old behaviors so willpower isn't your only defense.

**Day 40 — Your maintenance routine.**
*Idea:* Recovery is maintained by a few small ongoing habits, not constant vigilance.
*Practice:* Design your light-touch routine: a brief weekly check-in on your numbers, the occasional deliberate exposure to keep fears from re-growing, and a commitment to *not* resume checking even when tempting. Make it small enough to actually keep.

**Day 41 — Write your blueprint.**
*Practice:* Condense everything onto one page: your personal version of the spiral, your top triggers, the three tools that worked best for you, your flare-up protocol, and a short letter to your future self for the next hard moment. This blueprint is what you keep after Day 42.

**Day 42 — Graduate.**
*Practice:* Compute your final Week 6 average Spiral Score and compare to your Day-7 baseline. *(Retake the SHAI for your Day-42 score; compare to Day 0 and Day 21.)* Read your gains list and blueprint. Then name the real change — not "I proved I'm healthy," but **"I've become someone who can feel a sensation, not know for certain, and let it go."** Commit to the maintenance routine. You're done.

## Appendix D — Red Flags (Always Act, Regardless of Anxiety)
Build your personal list with your doctor. Common examples warranting prompt medical attention: sudden severe chest pain or pressure; difficulty breathing; sudden weakness, numbness, or drooping on one side; sudden trouble speaking or seeing; a sudden "worst-ever" headache; coughing up or passing blood; a persistent new unexplained lump; unexplained significant weight loss; fainting. *This list keeps the program safe: red flags get acted on; everything else gets practiced on.*

## Appendix E — Reassurance Script (for the people you ask)

So I'm asking you, with love: when I ask whether a symptom is serious, please **don't answer the question**. Instead, just say, 'I know this is hard — keep going with your program.' It will feel unkind in the moment. It's one of the kindest things you can do for my recovery."

## Appendix F — Quick FAQ
**"What if I'm actually sick?"** That's what your Sensible Health Plan and Red-Flag list are for. This program reduces the *excess* checking, never appropriate care. If a red flag appears, act on it.
**"Won't stopping checking make my anxiety explode?"** It rises at first, then falls — usually within minutes — every time you don't feed it. Week 3 proves this with your own data.
**"Is reading reassuring articles okay?"** No — consuming "you're probably fine" content is reassurance-seeking in disguise. The goal is uncertainty tolerance, not collected reassurance.
**"What if I relapse after Day 42?"** Expected and normal. That's what your flare-up protocol (Day 38) and blueprint (Day 41) are for. A flare handled with the method isn't a relapse — it's the method working.
