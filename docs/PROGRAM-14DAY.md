# The Symptom Spiral Reset — 14-Day Program
### The core reset: cut the symptom-Googling, body-checking, and reassurance-seeking

**Honest promise:** In 14 days, *start breaking the symptom-spiral* — measurably cut the Googling, body-checking, and reassurance-seeking that keep health worry alive — using the CBT and ERP methods clinicians use. This is the **core reset**; the full 42-day program goes deeper (see the end). It is **not a cure** and **not a substitute for professional care.**

> **Promise language (for marketing — important):** say "start breaking the loop," "cut the checking, Googling, and reassurance-seeking," "loosen the loop's grip and take back control." **Never** "cure," "break the loop completely," "permanently," or "for good."

> **How this differs from the 42-day:** Days 1–14 of the 42-day program only cover *understanding* and *cognitive* work — they stop right before the part that actually breaks the loop. This 14-day program is **re-sequenced** so the loop-breaker (Exposure & Response Prevention) is its core. The 42-day stays exactly as authored and remains the deeper, more thorough path.

---

## Read This First (Important)

**What this is:** A structured, psychoeducational self-help program built on the CBT and ERP methods clinicians use for health anxiety, with elements of ACT. It is a *training program for a habit pattern* — not a treatment for a disease, and not a chatbot.

**What this is not:** Not therapy, not medical advice, not a diagnostic tool. It does not replace a doctor, therapist, or psychiatrist; it complements professional care. If your health worry is severe, has lasted years, comes with depression, or significantly disrupts your life, do this program *alongside* a licensed therapist, not instead of one.

**If you are in crisis** — thinking of harming yourself or unable to stay safe — stop and contact your local emergency number or a crisis line now. This program is not crisis support.

**This program does not ask you to ignore your health.** Recovery is *appropriate medical care minus the compulsions* — not the absence of care. On Day 3 you set up a sensible health plan and a red-flag list. The target is the *excess* — the 3am Googling, the 40-times-a-day pulse checks, the repeated "is this serious?" texts — never reasonable self-care.

**Medical clearance:** Day 11 includes an *optional* exercise that briefly brings on harmless sensations (e.g., a faster heartbeat). If you have a diagnosed heart, lung, neurological, or balance condition, are pregnant, or are unsure for any reason, check with your doctor first and skip anything not safe for your body. It's clearly flagged and optional.

## How the Spiral Works (the loop you're breaking)

A sensation appears → your mind jumps to the worst case → anxiety spikes (and anxiety makes *more* real sensations) → you Google, check, or seek reassurance → relief for about four minutes → the doubt returns, often stronger. Every check quietly trains your brain that checking was *necessary*, so the urge grows. The engine underneath is **intolerance of uncertainty** — needing to *know for sure* you're okay. You can't get that certainty about your body. The way out isn't to win the certainty game; it's to **stop playing it.** This program: see the loop and make it safe → loosen the thoughts → **stop feeding it** (the core) → face the fear and sit with uncertainty → make it stick.

## How the Program Works

- **14 days, 5 phases.** 15–20 minutes a day.
- **Every day:** a short **Idea** (what to understand), a **Practice** (what to do), and the **Daily Check-In** you complete in the app (Appendix C lists the fields). The Check-In is identical daily so progress is measurable.
- **Three behavioral targets** run through everything: **Googling**, **reassurance-seeking**, **body-checking.**
- **Your Spiral Score** (below) tracks down over the two weeks. Your assessment result is **Day 0**.
- **Daily, at your pace.** A hard day done beats a perfect day skipped. Too much? Drop down a rung — don't drop out.

## The Spiral Score

Each evening, complete the Daily Check-In in the app; it computes today's **Spiral Score (0–100, lower is better)** for you. It's built so **resisting urges lowers it** — so a high-anxiety day where you fight the urges still scores as progress. That's exactly what recovery is: not the absence of fear, but the presence of resistance.

```
Compulsions  = (Googles + Reassurance + Body-checks) × 4     (cap 40)
Distress     = Peak anxiety (0–10) × 3                        (0–30)
Time lost    = none:0 | <15m:5 | 15–60m:10 | 1–3h:15 | >3h:20 (0–20)
Avoidance    = no:0 | yes:10                                  (0–10)
RAW          = sum of the above                              (0–100)
Resilience   = (Urges resisted × 2, cap 20) + (5 if today's exercise done)
SPIRAL SCORE = RAW − Resilience      (floor 0)
```
Track your **Day-0 baseline** (your assessment score), a **Day-7 checkpoint**, and your **Day-14 finish.** The trajectory is the story. *(Optional rigorous measure: take the validated SHAI on Day 0 and Day 14 and compare.)*

# PHASE 1 — SEE IT & MAKE IT SAFE
### Days 1–3 · Understand the loop, measure your baseline, and lock in safety

> This phase asks you to change *nothing* yet except to observe and set up your safety net. Forcing change before you understand the loop usually backfires. The behavior change starts on Day 6.

**Day 1 — Map your spiral (and notice the noise).**
*Idea:* Every spiral has the same five links — trigger → catastrophic thought → anxiety → safety behavior → brief relief → rebound. And a healthy body makes dozens of harmless sensations an hour; anxiety doesn't invent them so much as *spotlight* them. Naming both takes away their automatic power.
*Practice:* Write out your most recent spiral, link by link (what triggered it, the exact catastrophic thought, what you did, how long the relief lasted). Then record **yesterday's baseline counts**: how many times did you Google a symptom, check your body, and ask for reassurance? Begin the Daily Check-In tonight (Appendix C).

**Day 2 — The four-minute fix + your safety behaviors.**
*Idea:* Reassurance works — for about four minutes. Then relief decays and doubt returns, usually stronger. This is *negative reinforcement*: the brief relief trains your brain to seek the behavior again, harder. Checking, Googling, reassurance-seeking, and avoidance are all "safety behaviors" — they feel protective, they're the fuel.
*Practice:* Next time you check or Google today, time how long the relief lasts before the first new doubt — note the "relief half-life." Then make a complete inventory of your safety behaviors under four headings: *Googling/searching, body-checking, reassurance-seeking, avoidance.* Be exhaustive — you can't dismantle what you can't see.

**Day 3 — Theory A vs. B + your sensible health plan and red flags.**
*Idea:* The most important reframe in the program. **Theory A:** "Something is medically wrong that I keep needing to find." **Theory B:** "There's nothing dangerous wrong — I have a *worry problem about my health* that keeps me checking." And recovery is appropriate care *minus* compulsion — so we define "appropriate" once, and everything above that line is the compulsion we cut.
*Practice:* For each theory, write what it predicts and which better explains your actual life (years of fears that didn't come true, normal results, the way worry jumps from illness to illness). Commit to **living as if Theory B for the next 11 days.** Then write your **Sensible Health Plan** (one regular doctor, genuinely scheduled check-ups, anything a real clinician actually told you to monitor) and your **Red-Flag list** (Appendix D — the symptoms that warrant prompt care regardless of anxiety; build it *with your doctor* if unsure). From now on: red flag → act on your plan; not a red flag → it's spiral, and you'll practice letting it pass. *(If you'll do the optional Day-11 interoceptive exercise, flag any heart/lung/neurological/balance/pregnancy condition with your doctor now.)*

# PHASE 2 — LOOSEN THE THOUGHTS
### Days 4–5 · The essential CBT tools

> This targets the leap from *sensation* to *catastrophe*. You're not arguing yourself into certainty (a reassurance trap) — you're learning to hold the thought more lightly and move on.

**Day 4 — Catch the catastrophe.**
*Idea:* The catastrophe fires in a half-second, automatically. Slowing it onto paper breaks the autopilot. Health anxiety runs on predictable traps: catastrophizing ("headache = tumor"), jumping to conclusions, demand for certainty, "symptoms = serious illness," emotional reasoning ("I feel scared, so it's dangerous").
*Practice:* For one health worry today, fill a thought record (Appendix B): the *situation/sensation*, the *automatic thought*, the *emotion + intensity (0–100)*, the *behavior urge*. Then label which distortions are in it. Catch and label one more in real time today.

**Day 5 — Decatastrophize (and see how Google lies).**
*Idea:* The spiral lives entirely in the worst case. Widening the frame restores proportion — and builds *coping confidence*. Search engines make it worse: they surface the rarest, scariest explanations and strip out base rates (this has a name — *cyberchondria*).
*Practice:* For your top fear, write the *worst*, *best*, and *most likely* case, then answer the question that actually disarms catastrophe: **"If the worst did happen, how would I cope?"** (coping-focus, not proving it won't happen). Then, *without searching*, notice: Google distorts probability, so it can never give you the certainty you're asking it for.

# PHASE 3 — CUT THE CHECKING  *(the core)*
### Days 6–10 · Stop feeding the loop — Exposure & Response Prevention

> This is where the real change happens, and it's where you'll first *see* the loop loosen. You'll systematically stop the safety behaviors. Anxiety rises at first — expected and temporary. **Each urge you resist is a brick out of the spiral. Resisted urges are the metric of this phase.**

**Day 6 — Build your ladder + delay, don't obey.**
*Idea:* ERP works in graded steps. And urges are waves — they rise, crest, and fall on their own, usually within 10–30 minutes, *whether or not you act.* You can ride them by waiting instead of checking.
*Practice:* Rate your safety behaviors (from Day 2) by how hard they'd be to resist, 0–100, and order them into a ladder (Appendix A). Then, every urge today: **delay 10 minutes** before doing anything; name it ("this is an urge; it will pass") and let it crest. Log every urge you sat through. Start your **resist streak.**

**Day 7 — The body-checking fast.**
*Idea:* Body-checking (pressing for lumps, taking your pulse, inspecting skin, testing your swallow) feels like gathering information. It's actually rehearsing the fear and irritating the body — creating more sensations to check.
*Practice:* Pick your #1 body-checking compulsion from the ladder and **don't perform it for the whole day.** When the urge comes, delay and surf it. Notice that the catastrophe you "needed" to check for does not arrive.

**Day 8 — The Google fast.**
*Idea:* Searching is the single biggest accelerant for most people. Cutting it produces the fastest visible drop in spiral frequency.
*Practice:* Commit to **24 hours of zero symptom-Googling.** Make it easy to keep: log out, install a site/app blocker, put your phone across the room at night. When the urge spikes, use the delay + a pre-written line ("Google can't give me certainty; I'm choosing to let this pass").

**Day 9 — End reassurance-seeking (the people version).**
*Idea:* Asking your partner, parent, or doctor "do you think this is serious?" — repeatedly — is reassurance-seeking. Their "you're fine" is the same four-minute fix as Google, and it trains the loop just as hard.
*Practice:* Identify who you ask. Have a kind, direct conversation (or send the **reassurance script**, Appendix E) asking them to lovingly *not* answer when you ask whether a symptom is serious — and instead remind you you're doing your program. Then **stop asking.**

**Day 10 — Replace the ritual (and check your progress).**
*Idea:* A resisted urge leaves a gap. Filling it with a chosen action makes resistance far easier than white-knuckling.
*Practice:* Pick two **response substitutions** in advance — a brief grounding practice (slow breathing, 5-4-3-2-1 senses) for the spike, and a **values action** that pulls attention outward (text a friend back, step outside, do the next thing in your day). When an urge hits: delay → substitute → carry on. Tonight, total your resisted urges across Days 6–10 — that number *is* your recovery in motion.

# PHASE 4 — FACE THE FEAR & SIT WITH UNCERTAINTY
### Days 11–12 · Turn toward what you avoid; build the uncertainty muscle

> Response prevention removed the compulsions; now you remove the *avoidance* and learn, by direct experience, that feared sensations and situations are uncomfortable but not dangerous. Anxiety that rises *falls on its own* if you don't escape it — that falling curve is the lesson.

**Day 11 — Approach the fear (situational, + optional interoceptive).**
*Idea:* Avoiding hospitals, medical shows, health articles, or certain words keeps the fear oversized. Approaching them — without checking afterward — shrinks it.
*Practice:* Pick a *low-rung* avoided situation (read a general health article, watch a medical scene, walk the pharmacy aisle you skip). Do it deliberately, then **prevent the response** — no Googling or checking after. Rate your anxiety every few minutes; watch it crest and fall.
*Optional (read the safety note first):* for sensation-focused fears, **bring on a harmless sensation on purpose** and let it pass without checking — e.g., breathe quickly for ~45 seconds (lightheadedness) or jog in place (faster heartbeat). **Do only what's safe for your body; skip if any heart/lung/neurological/balance/pregnancy condition applies or your doctor advised against it. Stop if you feel genuinely unwell vs. anxious.** The sensation is unpleasant, not dangerous, and it passes.

**Day 12 — Sit with uncertainty (live as if recovered).**
*Idea:* Under every health fear is the same intolerable thing: *not knowing for sure.* You can practice tolerating it directly — and you've now built the skills to live from the recovered position.
*Practice:* Write and slowly repeat honest "maybe" statements: *"It's possible something is wrong with my health. I can't be completely certain. And I can carry that uncertainty and live my life anyway."* Sit with the discomfort rather than resolving it. Then spend the rest of the day exactly as someone *without* health anxiety would — no checking, full engagement; each urge: delay → substitute → carry on.

# PHASE 5 — MAKE IT STICK
### Days 13–14 · Lock in the gains and end with a plan, not a cliff

> A future flare-up isn't failure — it's normal, especially around real health events or stress. The difference now: you have a method.

**Day 13 — Map your gains + your early-warning signs.**
*Idea:* Evidence beats willpower, and relapse rarely arrives all at once — it creeps (one "quick" Google, one extra check).
*Practice:* Put your Day-1 baseline next to today's — Googles, reassurance requests, body-checks, peak anxiety, time lost — and write the change in plain language. Then list *your* personal relapse triggers (a news story, a scan, a season, a stressor) and your earliest tell-tale signs that the loop is restarting.

**Day 14 — Your flare-up plan + maintenance + graduate.**
*Idea:* In a flare you won't want to reinvent the wheel, and recovery is maintained by a few small habits, not constant vigilance.
*Practice:* Write your **5-step flare-up plan** (e.g., 1. Name it as a spiral, not evidence. 2. Run the Theory A/B check. 3. Delay and surf the urge. 4. Do the response substitution. 5. If it persists past a set time or crosses your red-flag list, act on the Sensible Health Plan — once). Design a light **maintenance routine** (a brief weekly check-in, the occasional deliberate exposure, a commitment not to resume checking). Then compute your final Spiral Score and compare to Day 0 *(retake the SHAI if you used it)*, read your gains, and name the real change — not "I proved I'm healthy," but **"I can feel a sensation, not know for certain, and let it go."**

## Success Metrics — how you'll know it worked (14 days)
- **Compulsion frequency** (Googling + reassurance + body-checking) down from baseline — a realistic target for a completer is a **meaningful reduction (often 30–50%+)**; results vary, and direction matters more than any number.
- **Resisted-urge ratio up** — you now notice most urges and act on few.
- **Peak anxiety and time-lost down**, week over week.
- **Avoidance down** — you're doing things the loop had been taxing.
- **SHAI moved to a lower band** (if used), Day 0 → Day 14.
- **Functional wins:** a symptom without Googling it; sleep without checking; presence with people again.

**Honest escalation rule:** if by Day 14 your symptoms aren't improving or are worse, that's not a cue to try harder alone — it's the signal to work with a licensed therapist (ideally CBT/ERP-trained). Build this into the product; it's the ethical move and it earns trust.

## What the full 42-day program adds (the deeper path)
The 14-day is the **core reset**. The 42-day goes further with: a **fuller ERP ladder** and far more response-prevention practice; **interoceptive *and* imaginal exposure** (facing the worst-case thought in imagination) done with more runway and support; **attention training** to undo body-scanning; **self-compassion** work; **worry postponement**; and a complete **relapse-prevention blueprint**. Position it honestly as *go deeper / make it permanent*, not as "the real one."

# APPENDICES

## Appendix A — ERP Ladder
Rate each safety behavior / feared situation 0–100 by how hard it'd be to resist or face. Order easiest → hardest. Climb one rung at a time; move up only when the current rung's anxiety has clearly dropped on repetition. *Resist* = don't do the safety behavior; *Expose* = approach the avoided situation/sensation.

## Appendix B — Thought Record
1) Situation/sensation · 2) Automatic (catastrophic) thought · 3) Emotion (0–100) · 4) Distortion(s)? (catastrophizing / certainty demand / emotional reasoning / jumping to conclusions / "symptom = serious") · 5) Worst / best / most likely + "if the worst happened, how would I cope?" · 6) One balanced alternative thought — then close the book (no certainty-seeking) · 7) Re-rate the emotion.

## Appendix C — Daily Check-In (every evening, completed in-app)
1) Peak anxiety (0–10) · 2) Time lost to health worry (none / <15m / 15–60m / 1–3h / >3h) · 3) Symptom-Google episodes · 4) Reassurance requests · 5) Body-checking episodes · 6) Urges noticed and **resisted** · 7) Avoided anything due to health fear? (Y/N + what). The app computes today's Spiral Score from these.

## Appendix D — Red Flags (always act, regardless of anxiety)
Build your personal list with your doctor. Common examples warranting prompt medical attention: sudden severe chest pain or pressure; difficulty breathing; sudden one-sided weakness, numbness, or drooping; sudden trouble speaking or seeing; a sudden "worst-ever" headache; coughing up or passing blood; a persistent new unexplained lump; unexplained significant weight loss; fainting. *Red flags get acted on; everything else gets practiced on.*

## Appendix E — Reassurance Script (for the people you ask)
> "I'm working on my health anxiety with a structured program. I've learned reassurance actually makes it worse for me over time — the relief is brief and the worry comes back stronger. So I'm asking you, with love: when I ask whether a symptom is serious, please **don't answer the question**. Instead, say, 'I know this is hard — keep going with your program.' It'll feel unkind in the moment. It's one of the kindest things you can do for my recovery."
